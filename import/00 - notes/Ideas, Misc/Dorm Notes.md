Don’t bring much other than the basics. Often, students will bring everything they can and then some. This leads to situations where there are multiple rugs, mini fridges, clashing decor, excessive furniture, silverware, trash cans, etc.

Pack minimally. Stationery, some snacks, bedding, clothing (even with clothing pack minimally until you know how much closet and/dresser space you have.), a water bottle (especially for orientation as you may be walking outside a lot), toiletries, etc. Once you know how much space there is, how much furniture, and closet/dresser space, then you will have a better idea on the bigger stuff.

Things to bring after you’re settled:: space may be limited so bring storage containers, in cabinet form but also just regular bins to keep to the side. Hanging racks to put in random places such as your bed frame, back of closet door (If the door allows), long phone charging cable in case you are on the top bunk or id the plugin socket is farther from your bed/desk, power strips, microwaveable meals that don’t need to be cooled, a blindfold cover for sleeping because you will have to share the lights, cleaning products (tissue paper, cleaning wipes, rags, etc), trash bags, trash for personal use and another for toiletries, bath mat for shower, soap, toilet paper, tissue paper, and I am a huge proponent of carrying small packets of tissue paper everywhere in case you find yourself in the bathroom without toilet paper, hanging chains so you can have more hanger space, etc.

Of course some of the items are dependent on how much space you have available, whether you have community or personal bathrooms, etc. but definitely find creative ways to utilize your space.

Other things you can do: You may be able to bunk or loft (higher bed without another bed below yours) your bed. If you have the opportunity, I suggest everybody lofts their bed so they can slide their desk and maybe even dresser underneath it. This really opens the room up.

Do not bring those LED light strips. They look nice but they peel paint off of walls and you may be charged for the damage. Same with command strips. Don’t bring them. Find other ways to hang stuff.

Use masking tape instead of scotch tape. It’s easier to peel off and see so that you know you’ve removed all the tape.

Reslife should reach out to do a roommate agreement with your roommates to set agreed upon living expectations. Definitely do this. And when you do, be as exact as possible. So if there is a question such as, “when is lights out?” Don’t say, “whenever”. Say give an exact time. Lights out is at 12am during the week and 2am during the weekend. This is very important, no matter how well you get along with your roommates.

Definitely do a Room Condition Report. These are to list all the damage that was already in your room when you moved in. Reslife will use this to compare the damage at the end of the year when you leave. If you miss something then you may get charged for it. Absolutely take photos of all the damage, not close up. Ensure it’s farther back so you can tell exactly where in the room the damage is located. It may be unnecessary, but you could also send the photos to your RD so that there is a time stamp on the damage. I have charged students for damage that was listed on their RCR. It was my mistake, but closing is chaotic and there are so many rooms and I miss things. Students have come back to me about these and I was able to remove the charges because of the RCR and photos. I have even removed damages that weren’t on the RCR because I had photos with time stamps.

Do not use a luffa. They carry bacteria that can give you all sorts of gross ailments. Please don’t use a luffa ever again.

Do not bring candles, extension cords, anything that has open coils (like space heaters, toasters, air fryers, etc). Review the student policy manual so you don’t accidentally bring something prohibited (or that you know what is prohibited so you can hide when leaving for breaks).

Know that Residence Directors (RDs) and RAs (Residence Assistants) are there to support you. Make sure you become acquainted with them. If you need anything at all, you can always reach out to your RD. They are likely to assist you, provide you with resources you need, walk you through situations like conversations with roommates, friends, etc. They are one of your best resources on campus. The RAs as well. They are students who understand what it is like to be a student, more than the RDs. They’re also some of the best resources.

Go to programming and join clubs both on campus and within your residence hall (dorm). This is who you meet people, have fun, learn things, and become more engaged on campus. Serious studies have taken place that show the more time a student spends engaging with their community, academics, extra curricular on a college campus then the more likely that student is to succeed in college.

Always read emails from the college and college departments. You want to be sure you are as informed and up to date as possible. Often, students will find me freaking out about something or another than had been emailed to them multiple times. They just never read their email. Don’t be that person. Your life will be much easier.

That is all off the top of my head. Please feel free to ask more questions if you have any!
